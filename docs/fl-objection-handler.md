## How to use this

Each objection has: the exact words you'll hear, the one-sentence pivot, and the full answer. Keep the pivot tight — let the prospect confirm they understand it before you expand.

## Objection 1: "I already have a property manager handling this"

**You'll hear it as:**
> "We have Jones Lang / Greystar / a local PM firm managing our assets. They handle this stuff."

**One-sentence pivot:**
> "Property managers manage operations. RealHQ surfaces the intelligence your PM isn't incentivised to surface."

**Full answer:**

Your PM tells you a lease is expiring. RealHQ tells you it's expiring in 89 days, the passing rent is 18% below current ERV, and the two comparable units that re-let in that submarket in the last 6 months achieved $27/sqft — which means you're leaving $2.50/sqft × 9,600 sqft on the table if you renew at the same rate.

PM firms are paid to keep assets occupied and problems off your desk. They are not incentivised to proactively quantify how much you're overpaying on insurance or undercharging on rent. Their model rewards stability, not optimisation. That's not a criticism — it's a structural misalignment.

RealHQ sits above the PM layer. You keep your PM. You add a layer of visibility you didn't have before.

**If they push back:** "Who in your organisation right now is looking at all five assets and telling you where the income gap is?" (Silence is the answer.)

## Objection 2: "How do you know our numbers are wrong? Where does your data come from?"

**You'll hear it as:**
> "Our benchmarks come from our broker. I trust them. Where are you pulling these numbers from?"

**One-sentence pivot:**
> "We're not asking you to trust our benchmarks blind — we're asking you to test them against your own portfolio. If we're wrong, it costs you nothing."

**Full answer:**

RealHQ's Florida market benchmarks are built from four sources:
1. **CoStar/LoopNet comparable transactions** — actual closed deals, not asking rents
2. **CBRE, JLL, and Cushman & Wakefield quarterly FL market reports** — aggregated and normalised per submarket
3. **EIA/DOE commercial building energy surveys** — utility benchmark data by building type and climate zone
4. **Marsh/Willis Towers Watson actuarial datasets** — insurance premium benchmarks by building type, construction year, and FL wind zone

Your broker gives you one market opinion based on the deals they know. We cross-reference four independent datasets. That doesn't make us infallible — but it makes the benchmark far harder to dismiss without evidence.

**The real answer to "how do you know our numbers are wrong?":** We don't. We show you where your numbers sit relative to the market. You decide if the delta is a problem. We just make sure you can't say you didn't know.

**Handling a specific challenge (e.g., "our insurance is fine"):**
> "What was your last renewal date? What broker placed it? We can run a peer comparison right now against 12 comparable FL assets in the same wind zone."

**Full answer:**

The platforms you're describing were built for institutional asset managers with dedicated IT teams and 6-month implementation budgets. They require data migration, ERP integration, and a professional services engagement before you see a single insight.

RealHQ's entire design philosophy is: can a single owner/operator get value from this without a technical project? The answer has to be yes — otherwise we don't ship it.

Here's exactly what onboarding looks like:
1. Upload your lease PDFs — our extraction layer parses them in under 10 minutes
2. Add your most recent rent roll (even a spreadsheet works)
3. We run the benchmark comparison automatically

You see your first gap analysis within the hour. No integration. No project. No consultant.

**If they say their data is confidential:** "You control what you share. There's no requirement to connect any external system. The data lives in your account and is not shared or used for benchmarking other clients without explicit consent."

## Objection 4: "Our portfolio is too small / too unique for a platform"

**You'll hear it as:**
> "We only have 6 assets. A platform makes sense at 50+ but not at our scale."
> "Our deals are too bespoke — a generic tool won't understand the nuances."

**One-sentence pivot:**
> "The sweet spot for RealHQ is exactly 5-25 assets — large enough that you can't hold it all in your head, small enough that you can't justify a full analytics team."

**Full answer:**

The investors with 200+ assets have in-house analytics teams, CoStar enterprise licences, and dedicated asset managers. They have this problem covered (expensively).

You are the underserved market. You are making $30M+ investment decisions based on a phone call with your broker and a spreadsheet that was last updated in Q2. The asymmetry of information between you and an institutional buyer sitting across a negotiating table is real — and RealHQ closes it.

"Bespoke" is true of every deal. But it's not true of benchmarks. A lease renewal negotiation in Coral Gables is bespoke. But the going rate for Class B office in Coral Gables is not bespoke — it's a market fact. RealHQ gives you the market context; you apply the judgment.

## Objection 5: "AI can't understand the nuance of CRE — every deal is different"

**You'll hear it as:**
> "This feels gimmicky. CRE is relationship-driven. You can't automate local market knowledge."
> "I've been doing this for 20 years. An algorithm doesn't know my tenants or my buildings."

**One-sentence pivot:**
> "We're not replacing your judgment — we're making sure it's calibrated against the market."

**Full answer:**

You're correct that every deal is different. That's not the problem we're solving. We're solving for: what does the market look like right now, and how does this asset sit relative to it?

A surgeon with 20 years of experience still consults the literature before a rare procedure. A litigator with 20 years of experience still checks recent case law before making an argument. Experience is the judgment call. Benchmarks are the calibration.

What RealHQ surfaces: your Brickell retail unit is passing at $62/sqft. The five comparable NNN retail leases that closed in Brickell in the last 12 months averaged $71/sqft. When that lease comes up for renewal, you walk into that negotiation knowing the number. Without the data, you're negotiating from instinct. With it, you're negotiating from evidence.

The relationship still matters. The negotiation still requires you. We just make sure you're not leaving money on the table because you didn't have time to pull the comps.

## Objection 6: "What's the ROI? I can't justify this spend"

**You'll hear it as:**
> "What does this cost? And how do I know I'll get that back?"
> "We're tight on overhead. I need to see a clear business case."

**One-sentence pivot:**
> "The average FL portfolio we onboard recovers the annual subscription on the insurance renewal alone."

**Full answer:**

Let's use your portfolio as the example. A 5-asset mixed FL portfolio of the type RealHQ serves typically shows:

| Opportunity | Average Annual Value (5 assets) |
|---|---|
| Insurance procurement savings (20-35% overpay on legacy policies) | $45k–$90k |
| Energy contract renegotiation (15-25% above market on legacy rates) | $35k–$80k |
| ERV gap (rent below market on expiring leases) | $50k–$150k/yr at renewal |
| **Total recoverable leakage** | **$130k–$320k/yr** |

Florida context specifically:
- **Insurance**: FL premiums are 3-5× the national average due to hurricane exposure. Owners who haven't market-tested their policy in 2+ years are commonly 25-35% above what a broker would achieve today. On a 45k sqft office in Miami-Dade, that's $15-28k/year of recoverable premium.
- **Energy**: FL has regulated utilities (FPL, Duke, TECO) — no supplier switching. The saving mechanism is rate schedule optimization. FPL assigns GS-2 as the default commercial schedule; many assets would be cheaper on GSLD or LP-2 based on their actual demand profile. Rate schedule mismatches on FL commercial portfolios typically add 10–22% to the bill. On a 45k sqft office running 20 kWh/sqft/year at $0.18/kWh blended, that's $18-36k/year on the wrong schedule.
- **ERV gap**: The FL office market has seen significant rent growth in 2023-2025. Assets with leases set in 2020-2021 are commonly 15-22% below current ERV. One renewal negotiated correctly more than covers a full year of RealHQ.

If RealHQ surfaces even one of these and you act on it, the subscription pays for itself within the first renewal cycle — typically within 90 days of going live.

**Close:** "What would it mean for your next renewal if you walked in knowing exactly where the market is — and had the data to back it up?"
