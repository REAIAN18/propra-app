## Post 1 — The MEES Problem (Day -3)
**Angle:** Most SE logistics owners know MEES 2027 is coming but haven't quantified the EPC D exposure across their portfolio. What that means in retrofit cost and lease void risk. Numbers-led, insider tone.

---

Something SE logistics owners haven't done yet, almost universally:

MEES 2027 is 14 months away. The standard moves to EPC B. Any commercial unit rated D or below becomes unlettable from April 2027.

Most older industrial estates in Kent, Essex, Surrey — built between 1980 and 2000 — carry a meaningful proportion of stock at D or below. Not unusual. Just the rating methodology of the era.

Here's what I haven't seen owners do: model the actual exposure.

Which specific units are at D or below. What retrofit costs look like per unit — typically £40k–£120k for fabric and services work on a 10,000 sq ft warehouse. Whether the lease structure puts that cost on the landlord or the occupier. What the void risk is if a unit comes back empty in 2026 and can't legally be re-let.

For a 5-unit SE logistics portfolio, the difference between planning now and planning in 2026 is the difference between a managed programme and an emergency.

The units that need MEES work are usually the same ones where we find the biggest energy savings too. They tend to go together.

Have you mapped which of yours are in the red?

## Post 2 — The Energy Contract Trap (Day -1)
**Angle:** Ofgem deregulated commercial supply but most SE industrial owners are still on rolled contracts from 2021–2022 peak pricing. What 15–20% above market looks like in £ across a 5-unit portfolio. Specific, credible.

---

Most SE industrial owners know Ofgem deregulated commercial energy supply.

What they haven't done: act on it.

Commercial electricity spiked at £0.40–0.50/kWh in 2021–2022. Businesses locked in at whatever rate was available. Wholesale prices have dropped significantly since — roughly 40% from peak.

The owners who retendererd at renewal are paying market rates. The ones who auto-renewed are still on 2022 pricing.

I ran a review recently on a five-unit SE logistics portfolio across Kent and Essex. Annual energy bill: approximately £600k. What a fresh commercial tender returned: £97k below that. Sixteen percent. Same units, same consumption, different contract.

£97k. Recurring. Every year. For not going back to market.

The Ofgem window created a permanent gap between owners who moved and owners who didn't. On a 5–15 unit SE logistics portfolio, that gap is typically £60k–£120k a year.

When did your energy contracts last go to market?
