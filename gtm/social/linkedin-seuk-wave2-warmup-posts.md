## Post 1 — The Developer-Retained Trap (Day -4)
**Angle:** When a logistics developer builds and retains, the energy and insurance contracts placed at completion are set for the handover period — not optimised for the long term. They never get retended. Hertfordshire and Bedfordshire corridors (A1/M1) have seen significant speculative development over the last 6 years. Most of those retained assets are still on completion-phase energy and insurance.

---

A pattern I keep seeing with developer-owned logistics stock in the Herts/Beds and South Essex corridors:

When a speculative scheme completes and lets, the energy procurement is set up at handover. The insurance is placed with the developer's relationship broker for the completion period. Both are necessary, both work, and both are then never looked at again.

Three years later, the asset is stabilised, generating rent, performing — and still on the energy tariff placed at PC. Which is typically 15–20% above what the same consumption profile would cost on a fresh commercial tender today. And still on the same insurance placement, which predates the 2023–24 premium corrections that followed the withdrawal of capacity and subsequent re-entry by Lloyd's and AIG.

On a 50,000 sq ft retained logistics unit in Hertfordshire or Bedfordshire, that combination is typically £35k–£60k a year in avoidable cost.

Not because the developer was careless. Because nobody's job it was to go back and look.

Most developers are focused on the next scheme, not the operating line of the last one.

Has your most recent completion had its energy and insurance reviewed since handover?

## Post 2 — The MEES Window (Day -1)
**Angle:** April 2027 is 13 months away. The MEES standard moves to EPC B for commercial properties. Most pre-2000 warehouse stock in Essex, Kent, and the outer Herts/Beds corridor is currently rated C or D. The difference between planning in Q1 2026 and planning in Q4 2026 is the difference between a managed programme and a contractor scramble — with corresponding cost difference.

---

April 2027 is 13 months away.

That's when the MEES standard for commercial property moves to EPC B. Any unit rated C or below becomes unlettable — new lettings and lease renewals both caught.

Most pre-2000 warehouse and light industrial stock in Essex and Kent sits at C or D. The outer Herts/Beds corridor has significant stock in the same range from the 1990s development wave.

Here's the planning window arithmetic:

A retrofit programme on a 10-unit industrial estate — insulation upgrades, LED lighting, heating services, potentially roof-mounted solar — takes 3–5 months to specify, tender, and procure a contractor. Then 6–12 months to execute across tenanted units without causing void or service disruption.

If you start the specification process now (Q1 2026), you can complete the programme on a managed timeline before April 2027 with room for slippage.

If you start in Q4 2026, you're competing with every other SE industrial owner who also left it late — against a contractor market that knows it.

The cost difference between a managed programme and a compressed emergency one is typically 25–40% on the contractor side alone.

The units most exposed are usually the ones that generate the most rent. That makes the timing risk commercial, not just regulatory.

What's your EPC profile across the portfolio?

## Post 3 — Energy and MEES Go Together (Day 0 or +1)
**Angle:** The units that need MEES retrofit work and the units with the highest energy overpay are almost always the same units. Poor fabric performance = high energy consumption = inflated energy bills. A coordinated approach — benchmark energy, execute the contract saving, then commission MEES retrofit scoped against the energy performance data — delivers better outcomes than treating them separately.

---

Something most SE industrial owners haven't noticed:

The units that need MEES retrofit work and the units with the highest energy overpay are almost always the same ones.

Poor fabric — single-skin cladding, no roof insulation, outdated heating — means high energy consumption. High consumption means a larger gap when you benchmark against the best available commercial tariff. It also means the retrofit work that gets the unit from D to B has a larger energy saving embedded in it.

This convergence matters for planning.

An owner who addresses energy procurement first — retenders the contract, captures the saving — has a baseline consumption figure to work from. The retrofit specification can be scoped against that baseline. The savings from the energy switch help fund the MEES programme.

On a 5-asset SE portfolio where three units are at EPC D or below:
- Energy retender: typically £18k–£35k annual saving across the estate
- MEES retrofit on the three at-risk units: £90k–£180k spend, typically 8–14 months of payback from combined energy saving and avoided void risk

Neither number is large relative to the asset values involved. Both go together.
