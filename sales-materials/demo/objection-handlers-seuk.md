## 1. "Our managing agent handles insurance"

**The reframe:** Managing agents administrate insurance — they renew what's in place and pass the invoice. Benchmarking the portfolio against the Lloyd's specialist market is a different function that managing agents almost never perform. Their incentive is continuity, not savings.

**Response:**
Managing agents are set up for renewal, not retender. Most MA-administered policies in the SE UK industrial sector haven't been competitively placed on the Lloyd's specialist market in 5+ years — agents renew with the incumbent because it's lower friction. RealHQ runs a structured retender across 12+ Lloyd's carriers simultaneously and earns only if we beat your current placement — 15% of the annual saving. Nothing if we don't.

**Proof point:** SE UK industrial owners on MA-administered policies typically pay 22–30% above what a direct Lloyd's placement delivers (RealHQ benchmarking, 2024 SE England industrial panel). On a 10-unit estate in Kent, that's typically £20k–£48k/year sitting in the renewal cycle.

## 2. "Our broker does a market review every year"

**The reframe:** A broker's annual "market review" is almost always a re-presentation of the same risk to the same carrier panel, not a true competitive retender. The renewal quote arrives from a familiar carrier and gets approved. That is structurally different from placing the risk fresh on the full Lloyd's market.

**Response:**
An annual renewal is not the same as a competitive retender. Most commercial property brokers use a panel of 3–5 carriers they have existing relationships with. The Lloyd's specialist market has 15+ carriers that price commercial industrial portfolios differently — particularly in Kent, Essex, and the M25 corridor where the specialist market has improved materially since 2020. If your broker hasn't run a competitive Lloyd's tender in the last 18 months against that full panel, there's likely a saving.

**Proof point:** Of 20 SE UK industrial policies reviewed by RealHQ in 2024, 17 had not been placed via a competitive Lloyd's tender process in the prior 3 years. Average premium reduction at retender: 24%. The broker relationship was maintained in 15 of those 20 cases — the saving came from the tender process, not from changing the broker.

## 3. "Our energy is fixed until [date] / We're locked in"

**The reframe:** A fixed contract locks you into a rate, not permanently into a supplier. The question is what rate you locked in at, and whether there's an exit or renegotiation right at the fixed-term end. Most SE UK operators on pre-2022 contracts fixed at or near the peak are unaware how far below their locked rate the current market sits.

**Response:**
A fixed contract locks the rate — it doesn't mean you're paying market rate. If you fixed in 2021 or 2022, you're likely locked in at 20–28% above what a current competitive commercial placement would cost. The question is when the fixed term ends and what your exit position looks like. For any contract ending in the next 12–18 months, now is the right time to prepare the retender rather than letting it roll at expiry with the incumbent.

**Proof point:** UK commercial energy peaked in Q4 2022. Operators who fixed at that peak are typically running at a significant premium to 2024–2025 competitive placements. For a Herts/Beds logistics unit drawing 800,000 kWh/year, the gap between a peak-2022 fixed contract and a current competitive rate is typically £14k–£28k/year.

## 4. "MEES isn't an issue for us — our buildings are fine"

**The reframe:** Most SE UK industrial owners know the current MEES requirement (EPC E, April 2023). Almost none have modelled the impact of the April 2027 upgrade to EPC B. Pre-2000 industrial stock — the most common profile in Kent, Essex, and Herts — is the most exposed category. Acting 18–24 months before the deadline is materially cheaper than addressing it under time pressure.

**Response:**
The current regulation requires EPC E, which most existing stock meets. But the April 2027 upgrade to EPC B changes the re-letting position significantly — particularly for pre-2000 industrial stock where envelope and roof insulation typically scores poorly. Addressing the compliance gap now (while there's time to phase capex across asset improvement cycles) is materially cheaper than addressing it at lease event under lender scrutiny or tenant pressure. RealHQ models the MEES exposure across your portfolio and integrates it into the insurance and energy cost picture.

**Proof point:** Pre-2000 industrial units in Kent typically score EPC D–F without a capex programme. Bringing a Maidstone-area industrial unit to EPC B (the April 2027 minimum) typically costs £40k–£120k per unit depending on fabric and services specification — versus a stalled re-letting on a unit generating £45k/yr passing rent, or emergency capex at compressed timelines in 2026.

## 5. "Our solicitor / agent handles rent reviews"

**The reframe:** Solicitors execute the legal process once a rent review is triggered and both parties are engaged. They don't proactively audit the portfolio, identify which leases are below ERV, model the reversionary uplift, or decide when to trigger a review. That gap between "lease event on the horizon" and "review in progress with comparable evidence" is exactly where income is lost.

**Response:**
Solicitors execute the review once it's triggered. They don't audit your portfolio to flag which leases are running below today's market, build the comparable evidence case in advance, or model the reversionary income position. Most SE UK industrial owners with pre-2022 leases have at least one lease 20–30% below current ERV — the issue isn't the solicitor's skill, it's that nobody's proactively modelling the gap. That's what we do.

**Proof point:** M25 industrial ERV has risen 35–50% since 2021 (CBRE/Savills H1 2024). A 12,000 sqft Kent logistics unit let in 2020 at £8.50/sqft vs today's market ERV of £12.50/sqft represents £48k/yr reversionary income sitting in the lease structure. Most owners don't have that number modelled — RealHQ surfaces it so you can use it at review, with a lender, or in a sale.

## 6. "Not the right time / things are busy right now"

**The reframe:** The MEES 2027 deadline creates a genuine timing pressure that doesn't apply to most other advisory conversations. At 18 months out, phased compliance is cheap. At 6 months out, under lease event pressure or lender scrutiny, it's expensive. The energy and insurance savings, meanwhile, don't require any particular timing — but each month on a legacy contract or renewed insurance policy is money that doesn't come back.

**Response:**
Completely understand — no pressure. The one timing note worth keeping in mind: if any of your SE England stock pre-dates 2000, the MEES 2027 position is worth modelling now rather than in 18 months' time. The compliance cost on a 12,000 sqft unit is roughly double if you're doing it at lease event under tenant pressure versus doing it in a planned programme now. Everything else — energy, insurance — I can pick up whenever suits. Happy to check back in [month — 60–90 days out].

**Proof point:** Building fabric improvement programmes executed 18–24 months ahead of a lease event typically cost 30–40% less than emergency compliance work commissioned under tenant notice or lender covenant pressure. (Savills Building Consultancy, 2024 SE England industrial survey.)
