# FL CRE Objection Handlers — Demo Call Reference

> **For Ian's use on 20-min prospect calls.**
> Six most common objections from FL owner-operators. For each: the objection verbatim, the reframe, a 2–3 sentence response in Arca voice, and a grounding data point.
> Last updated: March 2026

---

## 1. "We already have an insurance broker"

**The reframe:** Having a broker is not the same as having market pricing. Most FL owners have a single-placement broker who has not retendered the book in years — that broker's incentive is the renewal commission, not the saving.

**Response:**
A broker places your risk — they don't benchmark it. Most FL commercial books haven't been retendered across carriers in 3–5 years, and the FL market moved hard after 2022. Arca runs a structured retender across 12+ carriers simultaneously and earns only if we beat your current rate — 15% of the annual saving on insurance, nothing if we don't find one.

**Proof point:** FL CRE owners typically pay 20–35% above market premium at retender (Lockton Real Estate Practice Annual Survey). On a 45,000 sqft office paying $112k/yr, that's ~$28k sitting on the table. *(Coral Gables comp from demo portfolio.)*

---

## 2. "We manage this in-house / we have a property manager"

**The reframe:** Property management is operations. Insurance retenders, energy contract benchmarking, and income gap analysis are specialist functions that sit outside a property manager's remit — and most PMs don't have carrier-level access or energy procurement experience.

**Response:**
Property managers run the building. They don't retender your insurance across carriers, audit your utility tariff against current FPL commercial rates, or model your rent reversion against today's market ERV. These are advisory and procurement functions — Arca does them on commission, so there's no conflict with your existing management structure.

**Proof point:** Tampa Industrial demo asset: property manager-run, legacy utility tariff at $3.00/sqft energy cost vs a $2.00/sqft market benchmark — that's a $28k/yr gap that survives intact through any number of management transitions without specialist intervention.

## 3. "We don't have time for another platform"

**The reframe:** This objection assumes Arca is a software tool requiring ongoing input. It isn't. It's a commission-only service where Ian does the work and the owner receives a saving.

**Response:**
There's no platform to manage. You spend 20 minutes on this call, review the findings, and say yes or no to each saving we identify. Ian manages the retender, the supplier switch, and the engagement — you receive a summary and a saving. If nothing beats your current costs, you owe nothing and we've used 20 minutes of your time.

**Proof point:** Average engagement time for an owner: one initial 20-minute call + one 30-minute review call. Arca has processed the market data, done the benchmarking, and managed the tender before either of those calls takes place.

## 4. "How do you make money?"

**The reframe:** Commission-only is the most aligned structure possible. Ian earns nothing unless he finds and closes a saving. There is no retainer, no SaaS fee, no invoice unless the saving is real and delivered.

**Response:**
We earn a commission on savings we actually deliver — 15% of the annual insurance saving, 10% on energy, 10% on income gap, and a 1% arrangement fee on any financing we help structure. If we find a $28k insurance saving, we earn $4,200/yr while you pocket $23,800. If we find nothing, you pay nothing. Our interests are exactly aligned with yours.

**Proof point:** On the demo portfolio benchmark: $28k insurance saving (Coral Gables office) + $28k energy saving (Tampa Industrial) = $56k combined annual saving. Arca's commission = ~$5,600/yr. Owner's net benefit = ~$50,400/yr. Every year.

## 5. "We tried proptech before and it didn't work"

**The reframe:** Proptech platforms show you the problem — a dashboard, a score, an alert. Arca fixes it. The distinction is execution: Ian manages the retender, the switch, the contractor engagement. The saving happens in the real world, not in a UI.

**Response:**
Most proptech shows you a gap and leaves you to close it. Arca is a service — Ian runs the process end to end, from carrier quotes to supplier switch to lease benchmarking. The output isn't a report; it's a lower insurance renewal, a reduced energy bill, or a revised lease that reflects today's market. If the work doesn't result in a saving, it costs you nothing.

**Proof point:** FL industrial rents signed 2020–2022 are running ~21% below today's market ERV on average (Tampa sub-market: $14/sqft passing vs $17/sqft market = $84k/yr reversionary upside on 28,000 sqft). That uplift exists whether or not you have a proptech subscription — Arca surfaces and documents it so you can use it with a lender or buyer.

## 6. "Is this safe? Do you need access to our accounts?"

**The reframe:** Arca needs four things: asset list, insurance renewal dates, current energy bills, rent roll. That's it. No system credentials, no bank access, no sensitive login. Standard due-diligence material any lender or broker would ask for.

**Response:**
We need four documents: your asset schedule, insurance renewal dates, a current energy bill, and your rent roll. No system access, no bank logins, nothing that wouldn't go in a standard lender pack. All information is handled under a signed engagement letter with standard confidentiality provisions — the same framework a broker or accountant would use.

**Proof point:** The information required is identical to what you'd share with a mortgage broker or insurance broker today. The difference is Arca uses it to find savings across all three cost lines simultaneously, rather than one in isolation.
